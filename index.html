01 — Value Statement

What value does Starting Point offer?

Starting Point delivers value far beyond a shared meal. It creates a place where people find belonging, rediscover creativity, and grow into community leaders — all through the universal act of cooking together.

🍽️

Value for Participants

For people navigating social isolation — especially newcomers, young adults, and those facing food insecurity — Starting Point offers something money cannot buy:

  • Belonging: A recurring, structured space to form real friendships without pressure.
  • Skill acquisition: Hands-on cooking instruction, peer-to-peer knowledge sharing, and exposure to new cultures and cuisines.
  • Creative expression: Post-meal jam sessions, craft exchanges, and artistic activities that open unexpected doors.
  • Psychological safety: Low-stakes, playful learning where mistakes are welcome and curiosity is celebrated.
  • Economic access: Pay-what-you-can pricing removes financial barriers without stigma.
  • Identity: The transition from learner to contributor gives participants agency and pride in their own expertise.
🤝

Value for Volunteers & Staff

Starting Point is a mutual-aid ecosystem where contributors receive as much as they give:

  • Purpose: Cooking leaders and volunteers gain deep fulfillment through meaningful mentorship.
  • Leadership development: The participant → contributor → facilitator pathway builds real skills in teaching and community management.
  • Cultural affirmation: Chefs and experts can share heritage recipes in a respectful, appreciative environment.
  • Community ownership: Long-term members shape how the service evolves, creating shared stewardship.
  • Network: Connection with local sponsors and allied organizations expands professional and social reach.
🌱

Value for the Broader Community

Starting Point generates positive externalities for Seattle:

  • Reduces social isolation and its associated health costs.
  • Strengthens cross-cultural understanding through food and shared experience.
  • Builds a pipeline of civically engaged community members.
  • Creates a replicable model for low-barrier community infrastructure.
💛

Core Values That Guide Us

Five principles animate every Starting Point event:

  • Growth — Every meal is an opportunity to learn something new.
  • Fulfillment — Nourishing others nourishes yourself.
  • Discomfort — We honor the courage it takes to show up and try.
  • Passion — Enthusiasm is contagious; we let it spread.
  • Connection — Every table is a starting point for something.
02 — Research Insights

What we learned about reciprocal learning

To understand how a peer-to-peer skill-sharing service could support the transition from learner to contributor while fostering community, we used mixed methods: two semi-structured interviews, three observational studies during an art studio portfolio review and LinkedIn Learning workshop sessions, a competitive analysis of learning and community platforms, a two-hour co-design workshop, and AI-assisted research synthesis across four large language models.

2 Semi-structured Interviews 3 Observational Studies Competitive Analysis 2-Hour Co-Design Workshop AI-Assisted Synthesis
Synthesis: Participants were motivated by opportunities to learn practical skills and connect with people who shared similar interests, but they often lacked clear pathways to become contributors themselves. Existing platforms often prioritize content consumption or expert-led instruction, leaving limited opportunities for reciprocal exchange, peer encouragement, and community ownership. Starting Point responds by making progression visible: newcomer → participant → contributor → leader.

03 — Co-Design Outcomes

What the two-hour co-design session revealed

We ran a two-hour workshop with 9 participants recruited because they were passionate about learning and had experience teaching or learning new skills. Participants ranged from freshman to post-grad and brought skills including piano, fly fishing, dance, and other niche expertise. This range helped us see how people with different backgrounds approach both teaching and learning.

Workshop goal: Lower the stakes, prove that everyone has something valuable to contribute, and observe how skills move through a group when people are not formal instructors.

Co-Design Exercises

Icebreaker: Party Trick Share

Each participant shared a niche skill or party trick. This made expertise feel informal and personal, and signaled that unique lived experience mattered more than credentials.

Balloon & Dance: Teaching What You Just Learned

One team learned balloon animals while another learned a short dance. After five minutes, participants paired up and taught the skill they had just acquired.

Why: To see whether people could pass on a skill before they felt fully confident.

Drawing Telephone: Telestrations

Participants wrote phrases, passed papers, drew interpretations, and reinterpreted drawings until the original paper returned.

Why: To reveal how communication changes as it passes through different people and assumptions.

Sneaky Skill: Untrained Teaching in Action

One participant taught the group how to snap with all fingers. He naturally used analogies, hands-on assistance, and humor.

Why: To observe casual, unscripted teaching without formal training.

Spaghetti Tower: Collaborative Building With Different Goals

Two teams built spaghetti towers. One was told to build the highest tower; the other was told to have fun and make the coolest tower.

Why: To understand how framing and definitions of success shape group behavior, creativity, and engagement.

10 Insights from the Session

People can teach what they barely knowPeer teaching does not require deep expertise to be valuable.

Information degrades without shared contextTelestrations showed how quickly meaning shifts through interpretation.
Group dynamics shape participationWho led or disengaged depended more on dynamics than skill level.
Psychological safety is a prerequisitePlayful, low-stakes tasks made people more willing to try, fail, and ask questions.

Reflection

The session went well: energy stayed high, the mix of physical, creative, and collaborative activities avoided repetition, and the icebreaker helped participants feel safe enough to try and fail. The richest insights emerged during debriefs. Spaghetti Tower generated the strongest discussion about group dynamics, while Sneaky Skill revealed how untrained teachers naturally teach.

What We Would Do Differently

Future sessions should recruit beyond a university-centered age range, especially adults who may lose hobby-building opportunities after college and may need community more acutely. We would also record or transcribe debriefs so rich quotes are not lost while facilitators are running the session.

08 — Narrative Workshop

Role-playing the service in a real kitchen

Instead of making a polished video, we ran an in-depth role play with 10 participants, including ourselves, over 2.5 hours. We taught a brief history of empanadas, prepared ingredients, shaped and baked them, and ate together.

Key learning: Cooking acted as a social lubricant. It broke the ice, sparked conversation, started forming connections, and opened the door for creative expression and sharing.
Narrative Insight

The large, loud kitchen made high-quality audio and video difficult, but the conversations during cooking were more valuable than a polished recording.

Service Gap

Planning was the biggest challenge and should be detailed more clearly in the blueprint, especially for organizers.

Future Artifact

A journey map for the organizer would help the team understand staffing, prep, materials, timing, and facilitation burden.

Audience Shift

Inspired by a participant, we shifted the target demographic toward people experiencing food insecurity.

Service Positioning

Many services provide food; Starting Point aims to meet needs across Maslow's hierarchy by adding dignity, belonging, skill-building, and creative contribution.

Sustainability

The service creates enough community value to justify donations and grants that can sustain a dedicated space.

Where These Questions Led Us

Our central design question evolved from broad skill-sharing into a more specific service challenge: how might we create a financially accessible, human-centered cooking service where people facing food insecurity can gain practical skills, share cultural knowledge, feel dignity instead of need, and eventually move from guest to contributor?

09 — Design Principles & Requirements

What must guide our design

Six design requirements derived from research, co-design, and field observation — each grounded in evidence and tied to a core principle that will maintain design integrity as the service evolves.

Service Values

Community

Shared interests should build a strong user community, validated through co-design and survey feedback.

User Flexibility

Participants need agency and freedom to get what they need from the service.

Financial Accessibility

People will only engage if barriers are limited, so events should be free or suggested-donation only.

Accessible Experience

Digital materials should follow WCAG guidelines, color audits, and usability testing.

Engaging Design

Interfaces and events should be validated for satisfaction and meaningful engagement.

Trustworthy Service

The service must help users gain skills in a way that feels safe and credible.

Meaningful Experience

Participants should leave feeling that their time was well spent.

Perspective Widening

Starting Point should introduce new skills, cultures, and ways of thinking.

Design Requirements

1

Build Shared Routines That Bring People Back

"They always know to start with scales, so much so that I don't even have to tell them, and they'll sit down and they'll start."

Recurring, predictable rituals reduce anxiety for newcomers and build ownership for returning members. The service must establish consistent event structures that participants internalize over time.

Interview with Music Teacher
2

Foster Community & Belonging

"Space fulfills the need of… Yeah, like, belonging and, like, not being alone, and having a place to create."

Community-building is not a byproduct of Starting Point — it is the primary output. Every design decision must ask: does this help people feel they belong here?

Interview with Pottery Volunteer (P1)
3

Support Skilled Volunteers

"They need to know a lot to be a woodshop volunteer. It's not like anybody can do it… it's kind of a big commitment."

Expert contributors require onboarding, trust-building, and ongoing support. The service must create a clear pathway from participant to skilled, confident volunteer.

Interview with Manager of Makerspaces (P2)
4

Balance Instruction With Open Practice

"Students frequently interact with each other by sharing feedback, observing others' reviews, and building off shared critiques."

Learning deepens through peer interaction, not just expert instruction. Event formats must create structured open time for participants to observe, share, and build off each other.

HCDE Portfolio Review — Field Observation
5

Limit Barriers of Entry

"Even this, like, having free drop-in programs, like, that is mutual aid… and that is, like, a really big core of who we are."

Pay-what-you-can pricing, dietary accommodation, multi-language support, and welcoming framing are structural requirements that define Starting Point's identity — not optional extras.

Interview with Pottery Volunteer
6

Enable the Transition from Learner to Contributor

"There is a clear mix of nervousness and excitement, as students want to make a strong impression while also being eager to learn."

The most powerful moment in a participant's journey is when they first teach something to someone else. The service must deliberately create these moments and honor them.

HCDE Portfolio Review — Field Observation

Design Principles

P1

The Table Is the Interface

Every physical and digital touchpoint should feel as warm, unhurried, and human as a shared meal. We design for the experience of sitting down together — not for efficiency or throughput. If an interaction would feel cold at a dinner table, it has no place in Starting Point.

P2

Make Watching Easy, Asking Optional

Since people default to observation before participation, the space and the service must make it effortless to see what others are doing. Explicit asking should be one of many pathways, not the only one — and never required.

P3

Confidence Over Perfection

The goal of every cooking session, music moment, and creative gathering is to send participants home feeling capable — not necessarily skilled. Progress must be visibly celebrated over polish, and the structure of every event should reinforce this.

P4

Design for the Transition, Not Just the Event

Each event is one step in a longer journey. The service must actively design the pathway from first-timer → regular → volunteer → leader — not leave that evolution to chance. Every touchpoint should have a "what's next" built in.

AI Decision: Keep the Core Service Human

For the current design, we chose not to incorporate AI into the main participant experience because Starting Point is built around person-to-person interaction, human mentorship, community leadership, and belonging. An AI agent could help with sign-up or information navigation, but that would add training, maintenance, customer-service burden, and assumptions about users' comfort with AI without strengthening the core in-person service.

  • AI should not replace welcome, mentorship, or facilitation. A person should welcome someone to their first event, not a chatbot.
  • AI may be considered only as human-centered assistance. Future tools could support onboarding, recommendations, or opportunity matching, but should remain optional and transparent.
  • Human oversight is required. AI can misinterpret context, make mistakes, or reinforce bias, so significant community decisions must stay human-mediated.
  • Users need agency. If recommendations are AI-generated, users should be told and should be able to question, ignore, or override them.
